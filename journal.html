No. 03 — Testing

How to read a certificate of analysis

Every Nuvamin box ships with a certificate of analysis — a lab report for your exact batch. Most people file it with the packing slip. Fair enough. But if you ever want to check our work, here is how.

First: who signed it. A certificate is only as independent as its author. Ours come from an ISO 17025 accredited laboratory with no commercial relationship to us beyond the testing fee. If a brand's certificate is issued by its own manufacturer, it is a receipt, not a verification.

Second: identity and potency. The report should name each active ingredient and state the measured amount next to the labelled amount. We release a batch only when measured potency lands between 100% and 115% of label claim — enough overage to cover natural degradation over shelf life, never less than what the label promises.

Third: the contaminant panel. Lead, arsenic, cadmium, mercury, and a microbiological screen. The number to look for is not zero — instruments always have a detection floor — but a result comfortably below the regulatory limit, with the limit printed beside it for comparison.

That’s the whole skill. Three sections, five minutes, and you never have to take a supplement brand’s word for anything again. Including ours.

No. 02 — Formulation

Magnesium: a field guide to the forms

Magnesium is one element, but on a shelf it appears in a dozen costumes: oxide, citrate, malate, glycinate, taurate, threonate. The differences are not marketing — they change how much you absorb and how your gut feels about it.

Oxide is the cheapest and the most common. It is mostly a laxative with a mineral attached: elemental content is high on paper, absorption is poor in practice. It makes label numbers look generous, which is exactly why it is everywhere.

Citrate is a genuine step up — decent absorption, modest cost. Its limitation shows at higher doses, where the same osmotic effect that makes it useful for regularity makes it unwelcome at bedtime.

Glycinate binds magnesium to glycine, an amino acid with its own calming profile. Absorption is strong, gastrointestinal tolerance is the best of any common form, and the glycine carrier is a quiet bonus at night. This is what goes into Night, at 300 mg elemental — a number that refers to the magnesium itself, not the total compound weight. Read labels carefully on that point; many brands report the compound and let you assume it is the element.

Threonate is genuinely interesting and genuinely early-stage. The human evidence is a handful of small trials. We watch it; we do not sell it. When the data thickens, our position can change — that is the point of an evidence-led company.

No. 01 — The company

Why we make eight products, not eighty

The economics of supplements reward proliferation. Every new SKU is a new shelf position, a new search term, a new reason to email you. The industry average catalogue grows like a hedge: outward, in every direction, whether or not anything is there.

We decided early that Nuvamin would grow like a bonsai instead. Eight formulas, each earning its place, each reviewed annually against the current literature. A product that stops deserving its spot gets reformulated or retired — publicly, with the reasoning attached.

What we said no to is the more interesting list. Greens powders: pleasant, expensive, and consistently outperformed by eating vegetables. Fat burners: the honest ones are caffeine, the dishonest ones are worse. Hair gummies: sugar with a biotin garnish. Each of these would sell. None of them would survive our own standards page.

Restraint is not minimalism for its own sake. A short catalogue means every batch gets tested without cutting corners, every formula gets reviewed without a backlog, and every claim gets checked by someone who will have to defend it. Eight promises, kept, beats eighty approximately made.
